CyberArk Conjur vs. HashiCorp Vault: The Changed Calculus

This comparison looked different two years ago. CyberArk Conjur and HashiCorp Vault were straightforward: one came from the PAM and compliance world, one from the open-source developer infrastructure world, and you chose based on your environment. The IBM acquisition of HashiCorp in 2024 introduced a variable that didn't previously exist.

The IBM acquisition: what it actually means

HashiCorp changed its license from MPL to BSL in 2023. IBM acquired HashiCorp in 2024. Both events matter for Enterprise customers, but they matter differently.

The BSL restriction is narrow: it prohibits using Vault in a product or service that competes with HashiCorp. That doesn't affect most enterprise deployments. If you're using Vault to manage your own application credentials, you're not affected by the competitive restriction clause.

The IBM ownership question is harder. IBM's enterprise software portfolio and HashiCorp's platform-engineering-focused roadmap don't obviously align. HashiCorp's products were built for developers and platform engineers. IBM's enterprise software motion is built around large account relationships, complex procurement, and long integration timelines. Those are different cultures with different priorities, and it's not yet clear which one wins in the roadmap.

For organizations already on Vault, migration cost is high and the practical impact on most deployments is limited. For organizations evaluating Vault Enterprise on a new contract, ask IBM directly: what's the roadmap for the next 24 months, and who owns product decisions?

Where Conjur wins

Two scenarios favor Conjur clearly. First, organizations already running CyberArk PAM: Conjur's native integration means a unified policy model, audit trail, and governance layer across human and machine credentials. The operational value of a single vendor for both is real, especially in regulated industries where compliance documentation and audit trail continuity matter.

Second, organizations where procurement committee confidence is a real factor. CyberArk's enterprise security reputation translates to easier procurement in financial services, healthcare, and government environments where the buying committee includes people who haven't heard of HashiCorp but have heard of CyberArk.

Where Vault wins

Vault's integration ecosystem is still the best in the category. Native authentication backends for Kubernetes, AWS, Azure, GCP, LDAP, Active Directory, GitHub, and dozens of others. Dynamic secrets for databases and cloud providers, generating short-lived credentials on demand rather than storing long-lived ones, are more broadly implemented in Vault than in any competitor. For platform engineering teams building multi-environment secrets infrastructure that needs to work across heterogeneous systems, Vault's flexibility is not matched.

The community and open-source ecosystem are also genuine assets, even under BSL. The volume of community-built Vault integrations, the institutional knowledge accumulated by platform engineers who've been running it in production for years: those represent real switching costs.

What neither covers

Neither platform solves the credential discovery problem. Neither will find the credentials in your environment that were never put in a vault. That's the province of NHI governance platforms. Secrets management governs what's in the vault. It doesn't govern what isn't.

Verdict

Existing CyberArk PAM customers or organizations with strong compliance and audit requirements in regulated industries: Conjur is the more natural choice. Platform engineering teams that need integration flexibility, dynamic secrets, and a developer-native API across heterogeneous infrastructure: Vault remains the stronger choice, IBM diligence required. New Vault Enterprise evaluations should include explicit questions about the IBM roadmap, not as a dealbreaker, but as a changed risk profile that needs to be understood before signing. Both platforms should sit alongside NHI governance tooling, not instead of it.
