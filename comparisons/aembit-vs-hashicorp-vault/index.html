Workload Identity vs. Secrets Management

Aembit vs. HashiCorp Vault: Two Different Answers to the Same Problem

This isn't a feature comparison. Aembit and HashiCorp Vault take fundamentally different architectural approaches to the problem of machine credential security. Vault stores credentials and controls access to them. Aembit eliminates the need for stored credentials entirely. The right choice depends on your architecture, your engineering capacity, and whether you can actually operate either in your environment.

The architectural difference

Secrets management stores the credential securely, controls who can retrieve it, and rotates it on a schedule. Workload identity eliminates the credential. Instead of a service knowing a password, it proves what it is through a cryptographically-signed, short-lived identity document issued by a trusted authority. No stored credential to steal. No rotation schedule to miss. Aembit is a commercial workload IAM platform built on this model, using SPIFFE as the underlying standard.

The case for Aembit

You can't steal a credential that doesn't exist. That's the core architectural advantage of workload identity over secrets management, and it's not a small advantage. Every breach that involved stolen API keys, service account credentials, or misused OAuth tokens would have been prevented or significantly limited by workload identity, because those credential types don't exist in a workload identity architecture.

Aembit's policy enforcement layer goes further than any secrets vault can. Vault access control governs who can retrieve a secret. Aembit governs what a workload is permitted to do at the moment of access, in context, not just whether it can retrieve a credential. That distinction matters for least-privilege enforcement: a workload might legitimately be allowed to access a database in some contexts and not others. Vault can't enforce that. Aembit can.

The Microsoft ecosystem extension in 2025 added coverage for Azure workloads, Active Directory, Windows Server, and Entra ID, which matters for hybrid environments that aren't purely cloud-native.

The case for Vault

Vault is the right answer for environments that include legacy systems that can't participate in workload identity attestation. If you have application servers that authenticate with passwords, databases that don't support certificate-based auth, or middleware that was built before SPIFFE existed, you need a secrets manager. Workload identity doesn't help you with systems that can't prove what they are.

Vault is also the right starting point for organizations that need to establish a baseline credential governance program before they have the engineering capacity for workload identity. Getting credentials out of config files and environment variables and into a vault is a meaningful security improvement. It's not the end state, but it's a real step.

And Vault's dynamic secrets, generating short-lived credentials on demand rather than storing long-lived ones, reduce long-lived credential risk significantly, even if they don't eliminate it. That's meaningful progress in environments where workload identity is the right direction but not yet reachable.

The realistic answer for most organizations

Most organizations don't choose between Aembit and Vault. They run Vault for existing workloads and legacy systems, and adopt workload identity for new cloud-native workloads. Vault Enterprise 2.0's native SPIFFE support was partly designed for this coexistence.

The practical procurement question is which workload identity layer to use: Aembit as a commercial platform, open-source SPIRE built and operated by your team, or cloud-native workload identity from your primary cloud provider. Aembit's value is greatest in multi-cloud and hybrid environments where no single cloud provider's native workload identity covers the full surface area.

Verdict

Building cloud-native infrastructure from scratch: Aembit's workload identity model is the better long-term architectural direction. Significant legacy systems or mixed environments: Vault remains essential. The most common real answer is both, Vault for credentials that can't be eliminated, Aembit or cloud-native workload identity for new workloads. What this comparison should settle is whether your next investment should be in better secrets management or in starting the workload identity migration. For greenfield cloud-native environments, workload identity is the right direction even if the transition takes time.
