NHI Security Platform Comparisons

Independent head-to-head comparisons of NHI security platforms. Each comparison covers technical differentiation, deployment fit, and a direct read on which platform fits which environment. No vendor affiliation.

Use the vendor comparison tool to filter the full market before shortlisting. Use these comparisons once you have a shortlist and need to understand the specific trade-offs between two platforms.

NHI governance platforms

The most common shortlist pairings within the dedicated NHI category — both sides do roughly the same job, with different starting points.

Astrix vs. Oasis

NHI Governance

Astrix built its reputation on SaaS-to-SaaS OAuth discovery — third-party app integrations, token sprawl, shadow SaaS connections — and has expanded into cloud and on-prem coverage. Oasis started with broader cloud, on-prem, and IaaS/PaaS scope and has been closing the SaaS gap, with a stronger emphasis on AI-agent governance and outage prevention. The overlap is real; the difference is which direction each platform expanded from.

Entro vs. Clutch

NHI Governance

Entro pairs NHI governance with secrets detection across the software development lifecycle, tying every key, token, and service account back to a human owner and using behavioral-anomaly detection for remediation. Clutch takes a broader enterprise-visibility approach across cloud, SaaS, and on-prem built around its Identity Lineage tracking, with less developer-workflow-native detection than secrets-scanning specialists.

Secrets management and workload identity

Whether the right move is to manage secrets better, or to reduce how much needs managing in the first place.

Aembit vs. HashiCorp Vault

Workload Identity

Aembit's pitch is "manage access, not secrets" — secretless, policy-based workload identities issued at runtime, with nothing to vault in the first place. HashiCorp Vault, part of IBM since 2024, is the established secrets-vaulting and dynamic-secrets engine that most organizations with any NHI maturity already have deployed somewhere. This isn't really vendor-vs-vendor — it's whether your starting point is "manage the vault better" or "reduce what needs vaulting."

CyberArk vs. HashiCorp Vault

Secrets Management

Following Palo Alto Networks' $25B acquisition of CyberArk, completed in February 2026, CyberArk's PAM, Conjur secrets management, and Venafi machine-identity/PKI capabilities now sit inside one platform — a platform that also ships discovery and observability specifically for HashiCorp Vault deployments (Vault itself part of IBM since 2024). The result is an unusual pairing: an alternative to Vault, and a layer that can sit on top of an existing Vault deployment, from the same vendor.

Privileged access and category boundaries

Comparisons where the harder question isn't "which is better" but "are these even the same kind of product."

CyberArk vs. BeyondTrust

PAM

Both have sat in the Leaders quadrant for privileged access management for years, and the core comparison — credential vaulting, session management, endpoint privilege — predates either company's recent moves. What's changed is scope: CyberArk, now part of Palo Alto Networks, carries the broader machine-identity and secrets-management footprint post-Venafi; BeyondTrust's strength remains endpoint privilege and remote access, with a narrower but deep story of its own.

GitGuardian vs. Astrix

Secrets vs. SaaS Governance

GitGuardian started as developer-workflow-native secrets scanning — hardcoded credentials in code, public leak monitoring, honeytokens — and has expanded into broader NHI governance. Astrix started at the opposite end, with SaaS-to-SaaS OAuth and third-party app governance, and carries secret-scanning capabilities that aren't its core strength. Both now describe themselves as end-to-end NHI platforms; the difference is which end of the lifecycle each was actually built for first.
