Astrix vs. Oasis: Which NHI Governance Platform Covers Your Environment?

Astrix and Oasis are the two most frequently co-evaluated NHI governance platforms. Both have real products and real enterprise customers. The choice between them is not about which is better in the abstract. It's about where your NHI risk actually lives.

Where Astrix wins

Astrix coined the term non-human identity. That origin matters because it shaped the product: the detection depth, risk prioritization logic, and remediation workflows are most mature in the SaaS integration use case that drove the company's founding.

If your NHI risk is concentrated in SaaS-to-SaaS integrations, OAuth grants to third-party applications, and API key sprawl across Microsoft 365, Salesforce, GitHub, and similar platforms, Astrix has more signal, better vendor reputation context, and more mature remediation workflows than any competitor. That's a specific scenario. But it describes a lot of enterprise environments.

The AI Agent Control Plane, launched in 2025, was also early. Astrix recognized that AI agents are qualitatively different from service accounts, not just a new credential type but an identity that makes decisions, and built tooling specifically for governing autonomous systems. Whether that lead survives depends on how quickly Oasis and others mature their agentic capabilities.

Where Oasis wins

Oasis was designed with cross-environment breadth as a core architectural requirement, not an expansion after the fact. Discovery covers cloud workloads, SaaS applications, on-premises Active Directory service accounts, Linux daemon accounts, Kerberos service principals, Kubernetes workload identities, and secrets management platforms across HashiCorp Vault, AWS Secrets Manager, and CyberArk. That breadth is genuine.

Organizations whose NHI risk isn't concentrated in SaaS, where cloud IAM role sprawl, Kubernetes service account proliferation, and on-premises service account governance are the real problems, will find Oasis covers more of their attack surface without the same coverage gaps.

One credibility signal worth noting: Oasis discovered and disclosed a Microsoft MFA bypass vulnerability in 2024. Platform depth that generates real vulnerability research isn't the same thing as platform depth that generates marketing content.

The AI agent question

Both platforms are investing in AI agent identity governance. Neither is fully mature. This is a use case, governing autonomous agents that make independent access decisions, that doesn't map cleanly onto either platform's historical architecture. If you're deploying AI agents at scale today and need governance now, evaluate both specifically for the agentic use case. Don't assume that NHI governance depth in traditional machine identities translates directly to AI agent governance capability. It doesn't.

The acquisition question you should ask

Both platforms are venture-backed pure-plays in a category that large platform vendors are actively acquiring into. CyberArk's Venafi acquisition is the reference transaction. CrowdStrike's Falcon Fund is explicitly designed as an acquisition pipeline for identity security startups. Either platform could be acquired in the next 12 to 24 months.

Ask both vendors directly: what does continuity look like if you're acquired? What happens to the roadmap? What happens to pricing? That's not a hypothetical. It's a near-term planning question.

Verdict

Your NHI risk is concentrated in SaaS integrations and OAuth sprawl: Astrix is the stronger choice. Your NHI risk is distributed across cloud workloads, Kubernetes, on-premises service accounts, and SaaS: Oasis covers more of your environment and was built for that breadth. Genuinely mixed environment: run both against your actual asset mix in a proof of concept. Don't choose based on positioning. Choose based on what each platform finds in your specific environment.
