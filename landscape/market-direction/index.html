Where the Non-Human Identity Software Market Is Heading

The non-human identity software market took shape around a fairly narrow problem: finding the credentials — API keys, certificates, service account secrets — scattered across code, config files, and cloud environments, and storing them somewhere safer. That problem hasn't gone away, but it is no longer the center of the category. Two structural shifts are pulling the market beyond it. The first is a shift in scale: non-human identities have gone from being a subset of the identity problem to being most of it, which inverts architectures and products that were designed with humans as the default case. The second is a shift in kind: a growing share of new non-human identities are AI agents, which are created, granted access, and retired dynamically rather than provisioned once and tracked indefinitely — a pattern the discovery-and-inventory model wasn't built for. For buyers, the test is less "how many secrets can this tool find" and more "does this platform's model of identity assume things are provisioned and static, or does it assume they're generated continuously and need to be governed from the moment they exist?"

From secrets management to identity governance

The category's first generation solved a real problem: credentials were scattered everywhere, nobody had an inventory, and rotating them was manual and inconsistent. Vaults, scanners, and certificate managers gave organizations visibility into what existed and a mechanism to rotate it on a schedule. That was, and still is, foundational.

But an inventory of credentials is not the same thing as a governance model for identities. The questions that matter for a human account — who owns it, what is it allowed to access, has that access been reviewed, when should it be retired — were largely absent from first-generation NHI tooling, which treated machine identities as artifacts to be found and rotated rather than as identities with owners, purposes, and lifecycles.

The market's center of gravity is moving toward that second framing. Platforms are increasingly organized around the lifecycle questions rather than the inventory question — ownership and accountability for each identity, least-privilege access tied to an actual purpose, periodic review, and a defined path to deprovisioning. A platform built to answer "where are our secrets" and a platform built to answer "who owns this identity, what should it be able to do, and when should it stop existing" end up looking different in practice, even when both are sold under the same category label.

Non-human identity has stopped being the exception

For most of the history of identity and access management, the architecture assumed humans as the primary case: a person logs in, gets a session, and acts within it. Service accounts and machine credentials existed, but as exceptions bolted onto a human-centric model — provisioned by request, reviewed rarely, and largely invisible to the systems built to govern human access.

That assumption has inverted. In most modern environments, non-human identities now substantially outnumber human ones, and the gap is widening — driven by infrastructure-as-code, automated pipelines, service-to-service communication, and now AI agents, each of which generates identities continuously rather than through occasional provisioning requests.

The practical effect is architectural. Identity systems and processes designed as "human-first, with machines as an extension" are increasingly mismatched to environments where the reverse is true. The platforms gaining ground are the ones built around machine and agent identities as the primary case from the start, with human identity as one category among several rather than the organizing principle the rest of the system is built around.

Agentic AI breaks the discovery-and-rotate model

Earlier generations of non-human identity — service accounts, API keys, certificates — were provisioned: something or someone created them, and they persisted until someone deliberately removed them. Discovery made sense against that model, because the set of identities changed slowly enough that a periodic scan could keep up, and rotation made sense because a credential's lifespan was long enough to be worth refreshing on a schedule.

AI agents don't fit that pattern. An agent can be instantiated, granted access to several systems, complete a task, and be retired within a single workflow. The same logical "agent" might exist as many short-lived instances rather than one persistent identity, and an agent can itself create further identities for sub-tasks — identities that may never appear in a periodic scan because they no longer exist by the time the next scan runs.

This is not a new feature requirement so much as a new behavior pattern that the category's founding architecture wasn't built for. The shift it forces is from periodic discovery toward continuous, policy-based issuance — identities that are constrained at the moment they're created, by what they're allowed to do and for how long, rather than discovered afterward and then restricted. Of everything reshaping this market's roadmap right now, this is the shift with the least precedent in the category's first generation.

The category is consolidating into the identity platform

As machine and agent identity governance becomes a core part of "identity" rather than an adjacent specialty, it sits squarely in the territory of the broader identity platforms — the ones that already handle workforce single sign-on, access management, and privileged access for humans. Those platforms have an obvious reason to extend into non-human identity, and the market is consolidating in that direction, through both platform expansion and acquisition.

This raises a positioning question that applies to essentially every vendor in the category, not just the largest ones: is non-human identity governance a standalone discipline that will remain a separate product and a separate budget line, or is it a capability that buyers' existing identity platforms are likely to absorb natively within the life of a contract? Different vendors are making different bets on this question, and those bets shape their roadmaps.

Neither answer disqualifies a platform — depth of governance in a still-maturing category can matter more than breadth of an incumbent's bolt-on. But it changes what's worth evaluating. How portable is the platform's data — the identity inventory, ownership records, policies, and review history — if it needs to move into a different system of record later? How standards-based are its integrations, versus dependent on proprietary connectors that only this vendor maintains? A platform that scores well on governance depth today but holds its data in a closed model is making a bet on its own permanence that the buyer is also making, often without realizing it.

What this means for buying decisions today

Software bought today for non-human identity governance will need to handle a population of identities that looks different in two years than it does now — more of it agentic, more of it short-lived, and governed within a market structure that may or may not look like today's. That's not a reason to wait. It's a reason to weight evaluation criteria differently than the category's first generation encouraged.

Does the platform's model of "identity" assume things are provisioned once and persist, or does it support identities that are created and retired continuously — including agents that spawn further identities of their own? Is the platform organized around discovery and inventory as the end state, or as a starting point for ownership, access review, and deprovisioning as the actual governance loop? Does it treat human and non-human identity within one coherent model, or as separate systems that happen to share a vendor? And given the consolidation pressure on this category, how much of the platform's value — its data, its policies, its integrations — would survive a move to a different system of record, if that decision isn't the buyer's to make alone.

None of these questions make the original problem — finding the credentials that exist today — any less real. They sit on top of it, and they're the questions that separate a platform that solves where this market has been from one built for where it's going.
